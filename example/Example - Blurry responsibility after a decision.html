MATURITY IN PRACTICE / IA · DEMO CASE

Blurry responsibility after a decision

Ultra-short MIPractice_case reading (demo_001)
Date: 2025-12-04
Context: Organisational / team
Purpose: 2-minute live demo
A-band ≈ 4–6 · Mixed maturity
M-band (leadership) ≈ 5–7 · High structural responsibility
IA-Box → IA_risk
D-module off · dignity strain local, not extreme

Case snapshot

A decision has been taken in a team/organisation, but who is responsible for what and how it has been communicated remains unclear. This produces friction and irritation among those affected.

Guiding question:
How mature is the handling of responsibility and communication in this decision process, and does the resulting asymmetry count as functional or inadult?

Actors (roles only):

  • Decision-maker – formal leadership / project owner
  • Team / affected staff – implementers, people impacted

ACRPD – structural reading

A – Awareness
Decision-maker knows a decision is needed, but underestimates how unclear responsibility and communication affect the team. The team senses that something is expected, but roles and expectations remain vague.

C – Coherence
Formal act: “decision taken”. Missing: clear translation into who does what, by when, with which mandate. Words and deeds only partially aligned.

R – Responsibility
Responsibility is blurred: the decision is centralised, but practical burden and unclear follow-up are pushed onto the team without explicit allocation or support.

P – Power / agency
Leadership uses formal power to decide but not to structure responsibilities. The team has limited safe agency and no clear channel for questions or feedback.

D – Dignity in practice (short)
No direct humiliation, but low D₁/D₂ quality: people are left in uncertainty, which undercuts self-respect and relational respect by not taking their need for clarity seriously.

IA-Box – asymmetry check

T · Transparency unclear – decision known, but reasoning, responsibilities and expected actions remain foggy.
J · Justification partial – decision itself may be justified; handling of responsibility and communication is not.
TB · Time-bound unclear – no explicit timeline or review point; the unclear state may persist.
R · Reversibility partial – clarification is possible, but no structured path for appeal or revision is visible yet.

IA summary:
At this point, the situation shows a clear IA_risk: power takes the decision but does not take full responsibility for clarity and follow-through. With timely clarification, it can still be steered back into a functional asymmetry.

Key findings

  • Strength: a decision was made instead of endless postponement.
  • Weakness: responsibility and communication were not translated into concrete roles and tasks.
  • A-band 4–6: mixed maturity under real conditions.
  • M-band 5–7: leadership carries substantial structural responsibility for the friction.
  • IA-Box: IA_risk due to partial transparency, weak time-bounding and only partial reversibility.
Conclusion for practice
The quickest maturity gain lies in explicitly taking responsibility for the follow-through: clarify who owns which tasks, by when, with which mandate, and create a short feedback loop so that friction can be named and resolved.

Trajectory hint: without a clarification loop, low-maturity practice stabilises; with one joint clarification step, the pattern can shift back to functional asymmetry.
